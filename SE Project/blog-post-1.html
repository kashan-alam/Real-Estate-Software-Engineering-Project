This is How Humans Have Changed the World

In the past few decades, the world has seen tremendous engineering feats and transformations that have redefined our landscape and society. From monumental aqueducts to massive mines, humans have altered the planet in ways that were once thought impossible. Let’s explore some of these incredible achievements and their implications for our future.

The Giant Aqueduct: A Man-Made River

One of the most ambitious engineering projects currently underway is the construction of a colossal aqueduct in China. This aqueduct, designed to transport water over vast distances, is being built piece by piece. Each section is a massive framework of steel rods, encased in concrete, and weighs over 12,200 tons—equivalent to three jumbo jets.

Guang Aang, a 25-year-old crane driver, operates one of the world's most powerful cranes to position these gigantic blocks with precision. The water must flow north to Beijing without the aid of pumps, making the gradient of each section crucial. The entire aqueduct is expected to be fully operational by 2030, bringing fresh water to millions in northern China. This project is a testament to our ability to engineer solutions to meet the needs of growing populations.

The Bingham Canyon Mine: An Open Pit Marvel

While water management is critical, the need for resources has also led to significant transformations of the Earth’s surface. The Bingham Canyon Mine in Utah is the largest open pit mine in the world, producing enough ore each year to rewire every home in the USA and Mexico. The mine continues to expand, currently measuring about 2.5 miles wide and nearly a mile deep.

Matt Ler, the operations manager, emphasizes that everything we need that cannot be grown must be mined. This includes the copper essential for wiring, plumbing, and even cell phones. The ore extracted contains only a tiny amount of copper, necessitating the removal of vast amounts of material. Over its century-long operation, Bingham Canyon has produced 19 million tons of copper, highlighting the scale and impact of mining on our planet.

The Dirty Job of Sewage Management

As cities grow, managing waste becomes increasingly complex. In Mexico City, raw sewage is funneled into a single sewer system, where it can become blocked. Professional scuba diver Julio has one of the dirtiest jobs, diving into these toxic waters to unblock the pumps. This dangerous work underscores the challenges urban areas face in maintaining sanitation as populations swell.

The Shipping Industry: Giants of the Sea

To support global trade, we have constructed some of the largest vehicles on the planet—container ships. Building one of these colossal ships requires a workforce of 50,000 and vast amounts of resources. Each ship can hold 13,000 shipping containers and is assembled using massive cranes that can lift over 1,300 tons.

These ships revolutionized shipping logistics, allowing ports to handle container boxes in mere hours instead of weeks. For instance, the Port of Shanghai can manage 400 container boxes in an hour, handling millions of containers annually. This efficiency is crucial in a world where goods need to move quickly and reliably.

Urban Transit: The Lifeblood of Cities

In urban environments, public transportation systems are vital for moving large populations efficiently. The London Underground, for example, is the oldest subway system in the world, managing over 500 trains on 250 miles of track daily. It transports nearly 3 million people each day, demonstrating the complexity and scale of urban transit.

Tokyo's subway system dwarfs all others, accommodating around 8 million riders daily. The efficient management of these systems relies on countless officials ensuring that trains run on time, even if the commuters occasionally do not. The intricacies of urban transit illustrate how human innovation has made city living more manageable and interconnected.

Impacts and Future Considerations

These examples of human achievement showcase our remarkable ability to reshape the environment to meet our needs. However, they also present challenges. The extensive mining and construction projects can lead to significant environmental degradation, disrupting ecosystems and contributing to climate change.

As we continue to build and innovate, it is crucial to consider the long-term impacts of our actions on the planet. Sustainable practices and technologies must be prioritized to ensure that future generations inherit a world that is not only functional but also healthy and diverse.

In conclusion, the transformation of our world through engineering and urban planning is a testament to human ingenuity and adaptability. Nevertheless, with great power comes great responsibility. Balancing our needs with environmental stewardship will be key to ensuring a sustainable future.
